Music & cassettes

The soundtrack is a set of cassette tapes you find and play on the walkman. Each has an A and a B side:

    The WARD tape waits in a yellow box where you land in the Backspace; the others turn up out in the ruins. In-game music otherwise is a quiet synth bed (M to toggle).

    Down in the Backspace you can also recover deleted records — real sleeves the machines took out of the world (Van Morrison's Astral Weeks, Nick Drake's Five Leaves Left, Bowie's Hunky Dory, Boards of Canada's Music Has the Right to Children) — filed to the Scrapbook as covers, not playable, just remembered.

    Ideas & influences

    Ubik and its reality-spray, after Philip K. Dick.

    The Backspace a torn Ubik spray opens onto — an underworld after the Backrooms and the liminal-spaces genre.

    The console language's name and the older machine intelligence found in the ruins, after Robin Milner's ML and Joseph Weizenbaum's ELIZA/SLIP.

    "Vector theory" and the Magnifica Humanitas fragments draw on David's own writing (stunlaw).

    The shape of it — a long way home across a hostile world to someone still waiting — after Homer's Odyssey and Joyce's Ulysses. The one-eyed machines, the trick of being no one, the song of the towers, the dog that waited: quiet echoes, never spelled out.

    Books

    Some buildings still hold books. Read one (R) and the knowledge is yours for the rest of the run. Woodcraft, herb lore, tracking, endurance: each book teaches a skill that changes how you survive.

    Backpack

    A rare find in the ruins. Once you have one it carries itself: 16 more slots and a spare-weapon slot, filled automatically once your pockets are full. Eating (Q) and firing a gun draw from it too, once your pockets run dry — no need to shuffle things forward. Press I to see what's in it.

    Death is final

    Dying restarts the game from defaults — score, skills, kills, everything, wiped clean, and a freshly shuffled world to go with it, exactly as if you had chosen New Game yourself. Clicking away the Certificate of Death does the reset; there is no continuing the run you just lost, only starting the next one. Winning is the one exception — dismissing a victory certificate just lets you carry on with what you've earned.

    Staying alive

    Food drains all the time and faster when you sprint. Find tinned food in the broken buildings and berries in the tall grass, and walk over loot to pocket it. Health only recovers when you are well fed; starve and it drains away. A felled tree drops wood — but not to a penknife: it's too flimsy to cut timber, and trying just tires you out and nicks your health. A saw, machete, or axe-like tool does the job. Nights are dark and dangerous: find torches in the buildings to carry a pool of light with you. An emptied resistance cache isn't gone for good either — RON quietly restocks one every so often with fresh batteries, ammo or shells.

    Reloading the page without starting a new game isn't a clean reset either — it wipes your score and your entire obelisk kill record, just like a death would. Reload is only punished when it's used to duck out of the run you're already in.

    The animals

    Feral dogsHunt in packs; barking (!) means they have seen you. Hurt one and the pack backs off. Don't let them surround you.
    BoarsPaw the ground, then charge dead straight. Step aside: a boar that hits a wall stuns itself.
    VipersNear-invisible in tall grass; their venom drains health long after the bite. Where there are berries, there are snakes.
    RavensThey don't bite; they tell everything else where you are, and they steal food. Keep to the trees.

    The machines

    The AIs still control this land. Their black obelisk towers pulse across the landscape, and their T-class hunters patrol around them.

    T1wheeled hunter
    T2biped stalker
    T3ambush sniper
    W1revenge squad
    W2water droid
    W3repair drone
    W4laser hunter
    W5gardener drone
    M4report drone
    M5fortress sniper
    M6pack robot
    T1A wheeled hunter, faster than your walk. It cannot climb: put any rise between you, or lure it into a hollow and it is trapped for good. Sprinting outruns it.
    T2A biped stalker that walks exactly at your pace and does not tire. Sprint to break away, or stand and fight.
    T3Rare, and unlike a T1/T2 it never chases: a wheeled sentinel — a T2's trunk on a T1's undercarriage — that camps near its tower with a pair of always-lit orange laser eyes. It waits, unnoticed, until it actually gets a clear shot; the eyes flare, a charge-line joins them, and the twin volley lands at roughly double a W4's bolt, then a long quiet recovery. Get in close and it only rolls back far enough to keep the shot lined up; at point-blank range it claws instead.

    Every hunting machine — T1, T2, T3, W1, W4 — genuinely needs to see you: break its line of sight behind a wall or a hill for a few seconds straight and it gives up the chase and wanders off, only re-noticing you later if you wander back within range. Hiding is a real tactic, not just running far enough. The T3 takes this furthest: unlike the others it never notices you by proximity alone, only by an actual clear line of sight, so staying out of its sight lines in the first place is the whole game against it.

    Whatever is in your hand can be swung at them, even nothing at all — bare fists land a weak hit, a proper tool or blade lands a much harder one. Your penknife barely scratches them. RON — a resistance calling itself Reality or Nothing, whether or not it's still out there — hid weapons in crates inside the buildings: search them (E) for crowbars, bats, machetes, and if you are lucky the guns: a stun-gun (drops a machine cold for a while), an electro-gun (destroys a machine outright in a shower of sparks, scares off nearby animals, and its arc even scorches obelisks — a slower way to fell a tower than the OB-gun, but it works; its internal cell slowly self-charges while carried, so it never stays flat for long), a pistol or a shotgun (holes in anything, but gunfire ruins the salvage). Guns are dead weight without batteries, ammo, or shells, hidden in the same crates. None of them shoot through a wall — a solid object blocks a shot exactly like it blocks a step. Pulling the trigger with nothing in your sights still fires — and still spends the round.

    On defence, the crates sometimes hide shields. Just carrying one protects you — no need to hold it, and a faint deflector ring shows around you while it's up. A riot shield absorbs a laser from any direction, but it is only sheet metal: enough blows and it caves in to scrap. A rarer mirror shield throws the shot straight back to destroy the machine that fired it, but every reflection heats it — press it under sustained fire and it glows red, stops bouncing shots back, and finally melts into a lump of scrap (let the fire ease and it cools off again). Rarest of all is the forcefield — but it's the one exception that needs a deliberate switch: click it in whichever slot it's sitting in (hand, pocket, or pack) to arm it. Once armed and carried, a green energy shell wraps right around you that nothing — shot or blow — can get through, burning a battery a minute (faster under heavy fire, since every blow it eats costs extra charge) and pulling a fresh one from your kit when a cell dies; with none left, the field drops until you feed it one. Click it again any time to disarm it and stop the drain.

    Every machine runs on a battery: hunting drains it and they crawl home to their obelisk to recharge. One that gets stuck runs flat where it stands. A flat machine can be reprogrammed (R, costs a battery) — it turns friendly, follows you, and a friendly T2 fells trees for you — or, if you'd rather just be rid of it, it can be destroyed like any other kill: it can't fight back while flat, and it still drops scrap.

    The obelisks can be brought down. Carry a stun-gun, an electro-gun and a Wi-Fi block together and you're prompted to build an OB-gun (C): five burns topple a tower into a heap of circuit boards, batteries, scrap, and an access chip — every tower you fell always leaves a chip, so bringing one down hands you the means to jack into the others. A rare insane bomb can level one on its own (and it, too, drops the chip). Every tower is assigned one of eight circuit numbers when the world is built, so toppling every obelisk always gets you a complete set of one to eight — collect them and you can build a wave gun (C), which fans laser fire through a whole crowd at once. Clear an island's towers and you have broken its network — but the island is not finished until its daemon is (see The sea), and the run is not finished until every daemon is down and you have come home to Ithaca. Run out the countdown first and POSEIDON wakes: every surviving tower lights up and links to its neighbours in a web of bright blue lasers, and the factory throws wave after wave of W4s at you. But it isn't hopeless — topple a tower while POSEIDON is blazing and the whole web collapses, shutting the purge down for a reprieve, until a repair drone reaches the wreck and raises the tower again. Keep knocking towers down faster than they can be rebuilt and you can still win outright, even during the purge.

    Each obelisk has a small terminal screen on it. Walk up and click the screen itself to jack in — but you need an access chip to do anything. You get chips two ways: felling any obelisk always drops one, and every machine you destroy sheds a chip fragment — collect eight and press C to assemble a whole chip. Carry a chip (you don't have to hold it) and clicking opens a channel: a connect bar runs, then a live amber console — RON's own operating system, the same one the hilltop HERMES relays run — and while you're logged in the obelisk hides you from the machines. Click a terminal with no chip and you get the AI's own magenta console instead — a wall of moving data you can't use. Carry the electro-compass and click it to arm it — it stays armed until you drop it. Once armed, your aim marker becomes a small cluster of needles, one for each nearest thing worth finding, colour-coded: blue for the factory, green for an obelisk, yellow for a dropped backpack, orange for a dropped OB-gun.

    The console at the terminal runs RON-ML — a tiny language the resistance left scattered through the ruins as runnable scraps, half command and half operator's note. It is a little ML top-level: type a command and it runs, and a bare let x = e (no in) binds a value that lasts the rest of your visit, so you can build a program line by line. Reading and hacking need no AI key — the chip that got you in is enough. Type scan to list the obelisks on the wire, scan |> nearest to pipe that straight to the closest, name for the node you're on. loop node pins an infinite loop into a tower, freezing it and its garrison where they stand until a repair drone works the loop back out. hack node hands you that node's own key, and only with it does crash node key knock it dark for a while — let k = hack OB-XXXX in crash OB-XXXX k is the two-step spelled out. sleep 30, repel, and rewind 3 (only before POSEIDON comes online) buy you room, though they reach less far than they used to. The AI key (from a wrecked W-factory) is now needed for one thing only, the fortress: it comes sealed, so copy aikey to bring it into the console, decrypt it, and unlock with a hacked node key and the clean key together to drop a fortress key — copy aikey / let k = hack OB-XXXX / let d = decrypt aikey / unlock k d. That recipe is not in help; you find it on a scrap in the ruins. print aikey stamps a spare key at your feet, and an AI key never rots on the ground, so you can stash copies. The fragments you find teach the rest one idea at a time, and each real command copies itself into your notepad — a general page-collector: type notes, or press N anywhere, to flip through it (arrow keys, the ‹ › on the top bar, or the Contents drop-down). Find and read the RON-DOS Operator's Manual and the console starts autocompleting verbs — faded ghost text you accept with Tab (or → at the line end). map pulls up a schematic of the AI's whole territory; print map runs off a physical copy you pick up and click in your inventory to unfold anywhere. On a HERMES relay you can backup aikey and restore aikey: RON keeps a copy of your key off the AI's hardware, so a bad death needn't cost you it. Get something wrong and the terminal tells you what to try — it's built to teach, not to gatekeep. There's also a secret word, never spelled out anywhere — find it and the terminal drops you back into the world to watch what it does.

    The OB-gun's beam is just as final against a machine as against a tower: any robot caught in the line is wiped out on the spot, whatever its class — though, like any gunfire, it ruins most of the salvage. The limit is ammunition: every burn spends a battery, and batteries are better saved for the towers themselves.

    Somewhere on the map sits a W-factory: a huge 8×8 foundry where everything mechanical that isn't an obelisk-side T1/T2 is actually built, not at the towers. You can bring it down — hammer its hull with a weapon (a damage bar shows over it when you're close) or catch it in a bomb blast; after many blows it collapses into a walkable heap and drops an AI key, a way into one AI's mainframe (more use for that to come). Kill the factory and its waves and repair drones stop. Topple a tower and the factory dispatches a revenge squad of W1 hunters, tougher and faster than a T2, but strictly melee — a hit needs one standing right on top of you, not just nearby. W1s attack in real waves — each one closes in and strikes, then falls back to regroup, then closes in again — and a squad spreads out to surround you rather than piling onto one spot. They track a position triangulated from the obelisk network, refreshed every few seconds rather than live, so even a jammed Wi-Fi block only slows them down, not stops them. The factory also fields fresh W1 waves on its own clock, an unarmed W3 repair drone whenever a damaged tower is left standing, and a W4 laser hunter-killer — unlike a W1 it never closes to melee, holding its range and firing on you instead, both the instant you attack an obelisk and, independently, every half hour of game time.

    Out on the water, W2 droids hover in squads just above the river. Idle, they drift; but come near them and the whole squad turns and fires on you in waves. Their shots only reach you while you're in the water though — stay on dry land or a bridge and you can pick the squad off from the bank in safety.

    Deep in the south, past a hacked doorway and a maze, stands a walled fortress — the seat of one of the island's AI minds. It fields its own M-class guard. In peacetime only a drone or two patrol: the M4 is an unarmed report drone — it never fights, it just holds you in sight and reports the breach — and if it loses sight of you it heads for the spot it last saw you and sweeps there before giving up, so ducking behind a wall isn't enough; you have to actually move on. Slipping past its cone unseen in the first place is how you stay quiet. Get spotted and the fortress core rouses into a relentless violation response: it manufactures wave after wave and throws them down the maze at you. M6 are pack robots — they attack in threes to fives, closing to strike then falling back to regroup, and a lone one hangs back until the pack forms. M5 are snipers — they hold back out on the open quad and plink you with a low-power orange laser, annoying rather than deadly. Break the core's hull down (heavy kit only) and the AI dies: every machine on the whole island powers down where it stands — and every obelisk goes dark with it.

    Weapon strikes on any machine now throw a shower of sparks on impact.

    Bombs

    Scavenged timed bombs come in four sizes — small, medium, large, and a rare insane one. Hold a bomb and use it (/ or left click) to throw it, ticking, a good distance out — thrown in an arc, so it clears a wall or a low block between you and the target instead of stopping dead at the first one, useful for reaching a W4 that's holed up behind cover. Then get clear. When the fuse runs out it goes off in a cloud of fire that hurts everything nearby — animals, machines, droids, and you. The insane bomb is fierce enough to bring down an obelisk.

    The world

    A river divides the map; the bridges are the only crossings. Streams can be waded, slowly. Away from the towns the ground gets properly rugged — steep, tall hills and deep hollows, not just gentle rises — and high ground costs stamina to climb. Rubble and rocks can be climbed too, one step up like any other rise, and walked across the top of once you're up there — building walls stay solid, so a town or a house is still a real boundary, not just another slope. Somewhere in the ruins, the story of what happened here is waiting to be found. Sprayed slogans turn up here and there on the walls — some anti-machine, some signed by RON — and abandoned cars sit dead on the roads where the grid failed them.

    Ubik & the tear

    A battered can of Ubik turns up somewhere in the ruins. Each spray brightens a patch of ground and everything on it, warmer and more real, fading back after a while — and any hunting machine that wanders into the bright patch loses its mind for as long as it lingers, spinning and juddering and swinging at whatever other machine strays close instead of hunting you. Spray the same spot three times and it does something else: it tears clean through, and holds open. Step into the tear and you fall out of the world entirely, into the Backspace — a vast, humming, fluorescent-lit sprawl: huge rooms of every size scattered across an endless sea of worn yellow floor, joined by road-worn corridors, furniture stacked around at random, the odd room washed a wrong baby-blue, strip-lights flickering overhead. Something pale wanders the far rooms; it only comes for you if it actually sees you. The way back is a plain door set in the wall of the room you arrive in — walk up to it to leave. It being such an ordinary door down here is the unsettling part. Wander deeper, toward the thing and the cassette left in the farthest room, at your own risk; keep your eyes on it and it can't reach you.

    Walkman

    You carry a walkman on a strap — its slot sits on the dashboard. It takes cassette tapes; you start with one (meme's "compilation"), and more turn up out in the ruins and down in the Backspace. Click the tape in the walkman to play it: once for side A, again to flip to side B, again to stop. Each side can hold more than one track and loops round. The reels turn while it plays and the artist and track drift slowly across the little screen below. The game's own music is just a quiet synth bed (or off, toggled with M) — the only way to hear the tapes is to start one on the walkman.

    Ogygia, and getting off it

    You wake washed up on OGYGIA, and it is not your island. It belongs to CALYPSO, and she keeps you here — well, and kindly, and completely. Hers is the one island whose daemon you do not kill: her core cannot be broken, and her guards detain rather than finish you. The win on Ogygia is leaving, which turns out to be harder.

    Two things stand between you and the water. The first is her: go into the fortress, reach the terminal on the core itself, and refunction her with the right virus in hand — she is soporific, and will deflect almost anything you say until you have the thunder to say it with. The second is the boat. A raft lashed together out of wood is not a ship, and the sea knows it: launch one and Poseidon rows you back onto your own sand, every time. Refunctioning her hands you her shipwright's recipe, the golden axe, and with it you can build a sea-worthy greek ship from wood and three found parts — a sail at a beached wreck, an oar and a rope left in the fishermen's huts. Only the greek ship survives the crossing.

    Sailing

    Board a beached boat and you do not pick a destination off a menu. You row out first: the land slides away behind you, the fog closes ahead, and only when there is no land in any direction does the chart open and ask which way you put the bow. Each landfall is listed with its Homeric epithet, the formula the poem itself uses. Change your mind and you can come about and pull back for the beach. You leave every later island in the ship you arrived in, so the sea only refuses you once, on Ogygia.

    The archipelago

    OGYGIACalypso's island, where you were kept, and kept well. The one you leave rather than win.
    AEGILIAThe goat isle, harbourless — the land of the Cyclopes. POLYPHEMUS is a single vast eye, and it watches by genuine line of sight. A great mountain stands over it, rock above the tree line and a patchy snow-cap wearing a cloud.
    AEAEAWhere the dawn has her dancing-floor. CIRCE does not kill what she takes, she changes what it is — stay ashore and she begins, very gently, to rewrite you. Moly holds you as you are; it grows where HERMES stands.
    THRINACIAThe island of the Sun. HELIOS's cattle graze the headland, golden and unafraid, and they are forbidden. Take one and the island is your enemy to the end.
    ITHACAClear-seen, a good nurse of young men. Home — rough, and small, and yours, if the sea will let you come to it.

    Each island's card is keyed to its own daemon, so one virus does not open the whole archipelago: you forge a payload per island at its HERMES relay. Landing somewhere for the first time sets a checkpoint you can load back to from the title.

    Scylla and Charybdis

    The passage between AEAEA and THRINACIA runs through a throat of rock, and the sea makes you choose. Scylla is the cliff: hug it and six necks come down and take a few things off your deck — a certain toll, paid once, and you are through. Charybdis is the whirlpool: steer wide of the rock and chance her instead. Usually she only mauls you. Sometimes she takes the boat and the voyage with it, and puts you back on the beach you sailed from. It is a real bargain — a bounded loss against an unbounded one — and neither of them will kill you outright. If you have made landfall on Aeaea, Circe will text you before the narrows and tell you which to take. If you have never been to her island, you choose blind.

    The phone

    You carry a Nokia 3310 (O). On Ogygia it is Calypso's channel — she texts you warnings, tips and pleas, and while her hold on you is warm she will freeze a machine bearing down on you. Off her island the handset finds whichever AI rules the ground you are standing on, and you can text it: POLYPHEMUS answers in blunt capitals, CIRCE sweetly and in the language of tariffs, HELIOS like the sun itself. Signal comes off the island's own network, so it is strongest near a fortress core and gone entirely where there is nothing left to carry it. Making landfall anywhere new brings a roaming welcome from a carrier that no longer has customers, only subjects. There is also Snake on it, and it remembers your best game.

    The road of the dead

    The Backspace is an alternative way across. Its ways up are labelled doors, one per island, each drawn with its name on an isometric exit sign — walk up to the door of the island you want and you come up there. No chart, no boat: the doors are the choice.

    Coming home

    Felling an island's daemon powers down every machine and every obelisk on that island. Do that on all four of the martial islands — and leave Calypso rather than kill her — then sail for Ithaca. Land there with the war won and Argos lifts his grey head and knows you, and that is the end of the road and the beginning of the rest of it. Land there with daemons still standing and it is only landfall: the machines still hold the sea. Fell the rest of them, then come back for good.
