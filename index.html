Books

Some buildings still hold books. Read one (R) and the knowledge is yours for the rest of the run. Woodcraft, herb lore, tracking, endurance: each book teaches a skill that changes how you survive.

Backpack

A rare find in the ruins. Once you have one it carries itself: 16 more slots and a spare-weapon slot, filled automatically once your pockets are full. Eating (Q) and firing a gun draw from it too, once your pockets run dry — no need to shuffle things forward. Press I to see what's in it.

Death is final

Dying restarts the game from defaults — score, skills, kills, everything, wiped clean, and a freshly shuffled world to go with it, exactly as if you had chosen New Game yourself. Clicking away the Certificate of Death does the reset; there is no continuing the run you just lost, only starting the next one. Winning is the one exception — dismissing a victory certificate just lets you carry on with what you've earned.

Staying alive

Food drains all the time and faster when you sprint. Find tinned food in the broken buildings and berries in the tall grass, and walk over loot to pocket it. Health only recovers when you are well fed; starve and it drains away. A felled tree drops wood — but not to a penknife: it's too flimsy to cut timber, and trying just tires you out and nicks your health. A saw, machete, or axe-like tool does the job. Nights are dark and dangerous: find torches in the buildings to carry a pool of light with you. An emptied resistance cache isn't gone for good either — RON quietly restocks one every so often with fresh batteries, ammo or shells.

Reloading the page without starting a new game isn't a clean reset either — it wipes your score and your entire obelisk kill record, just like a death would. Reload is only punished when it's used to duck out of the run you're already in.

The animals

Feral dogsHunt in packs; barking (!) means they have seen you. Hurt one and the pack backs off. Don't let them surround you.
BoarsPaw the ground, then charge dead straight. Step aside: a boar that hits a wall stuns itself.
VipersNear-invisible in tall grass; their venom drains health long after the bite. Where there are berries, there are snakes.
RavensThey don't bite; they tell everything else where you are, and they steal food. Keep to the trees.

The machines

The AIs still control this land. Their black obelisk towers pulse across the landscape, and their T-class hunters patrol around them.

T2biped stalker
W1revenge squad
W2water droid
W3repair drone
W4laser hunter
T1A wheeled hunter, faster than your walk. It cannot climb: put any rise between you, or lure it into a hollow and it is trapped for good. Sprinting outruns it.
T2A biped stalker that walks exactly at your pace and does not tire. Sprint to break away, or stand and fight.

Every hunting machine — T1, T2, W1, W4 — genuinely needs to see you: break its line of sight behind a wall or a hill for a few seconds straight and it gives up the chase and wanders off, only re-noticing you later if you wander back within range. Hiding is a real tactic, not just running far enough.

Whatever is in your hand can be swung at them, even nothing at all — bare fists land a weak hit, a proper tool or blade lands a much harder one. Your penknife barely scratches them. RON — a resistance calling itself Reality or Nothing, whether or not it's still out there — hid weapons in crates inside the buildings: search them (E) for crowbars, bats, machetes, and if you are lucky the guns: a stun-gun (drops a machine cold for a while), an electro-gun (destroys a machine outright in a shower of sparks, scares off nearby animals, and its arc even scorches obelisks — a slower way to fell a tower than the OB-gun, but it works; its internal cell slowly self-charges while carried, so it never stays flat for long), a pistol or a shotgun (holes in anything, but gunfire ruins the salvage). Guns are dead weight without batteries, ammo, or shells, hidden in the same crates. None of them shoot through a wall — a solid object blocks a shot exactly like it blocks a step. Pulling the trigger with nothing in your sights still fires — and still spends the round.

On defence, the crates sometimes hide shields. Just carrying one protects you — no need to hold it, and a faint deflector ring shows around you while it's up. A riot shield absorbs a laser from any direction; a rarer mirror shield throws it straight back to destroy the machine that fired it. Rarest of all is the forcefield — but it's the one exception that needs a deliberate switch: click it in whichever slot it's sitting in (hand, pocket, or pack) to arm it. Once armed and carried, a green energy shell wraps right around you that nothing — shot or blow — can get through, burning a battery a minute and pulling a fresh one from your kit when a cell dies; with none left, the field drops until you feed it one. Click it again any time to disarm it and stop the drain.

Every machine runs on a battery: hunting drains it and they crawl home to their obelisk to recharge. One that gets stuck runs flat where it stands. A flat machine can be reprogrammed (R, costs a battery) — it turns friendly, follows you, and a friendly T2 fells trees for you — or, if you'd rather just be rid of it, it can be destroyed like any other kill: it can't fight back while flat, and it still drops scrap.

The obelisks can be brought down. Carry a stun-gun, an electro-gun and a Wi-Fi block together and you're prompted to build an OB-gun (C): five burns topple a tower into a heap of circuit boards, batteries, scrap, and an access chip — every tower you fell always leaves a chip, so bringing one down hands you the means to jack into the others. A rare insane bomb can level one on its own (and it, too, drops the chip). Every tower is assigned one of eight circuit numbers when the world is built, so toppling every obelisk always gets you a complete set of one to eight — collect them and you can build a wave gun (C), which fans laser fire through a whole crowd at once. Bring down every obelisk on the map and you win — the AI is defeated before SKYLINK ever wakes. Run out the countdown first and it wakes instead: every surviving tower lights up and links to its neighbours in a web of bright blue lasers, and the factory throws wave after wave of W4s at you. But it isn't hopeless — topple a tower while SKYLINK is blazing and the whole web collapses, shutting the purge down for a reprieve, until a repair drone reaches the wreck and raises the tower again. Keep knocking towers down faster than they can be rebuilt and you can still win outright, even during the purge.

Each obelisk has a small green terminal screen on it. Walk up and click the screen itself to jack in — but you need an access chip to do anything. You get chips two ways: felling any obelisk always drops one, and every machine you destroy sheds a chip fragment — collect eight and press C to assemble a whole chip. Carry a chip (you don't have to hold it) and clicking opens a channel: a connect bar runs, then a live console prompt, and while you're logged in the obelisk hides you from the machines. Click a terminal with no chip and you get the AI's own console instead — a wall of moving data you can't use. Carry the electro-compass and click it to arm it — it stays armed until you drop it. Once armed, your aim marker becomes a small cluster of needles, one for each nearest thing worth finding, colour-coded: blue for the factory, green for an obelisk, yellow for a dropped backpack, orange for a dropped OB-gun.

The console at the terminal runs RON-ML — a tiny language the resistance left scattered through the ruins as runnable scraps, half command and half operator's note. Type scan to list the obelisks on the wire, scan |> nearest to pipe that straight to the closest one. With an AI key (dropped by a destroyed W-factory) you can go further: hack node hands you that node's own key, and only with that key does crash node key knock it dark for a while (a repair drone will eventually raise it again — this is a tactic, not a substitute for actually toppling a tower). let k = hack OB-XXXX in crash OB-XXXX k is the whole two-step spelled out; the fragments you find teach it one idea at a time. sleep 30 and repel (both need the key too) buy you a quiet minute nearby. Get something wrong and the terminal tells you what to try instead — it's built to teach, not to gatekeep. There's also a secret word, never spelled out anywhere — find it and the terminal will drop you straight back into the world to watch what it does.

Careful with the OB-gun's beam, though: fired at a robot rather than a tower, it doesn't kill — it corrupts the machine into a zombie, marked by a sickly green glow. A zombie shrugs off every weapon in the game except the bow and the wave gun; anything else just bounces off. W1 hunters (below) are already the AI's own revenge squad and can't be corrupted this way — the beam just damages them like anything else.

Somewhere on the map sits a W-factory: a huge 8×8 foundry where everything mechanical that isn't an obelisk-side T1/T2 is actually built, not at the towers. You can bring it down — hammer its hull with a weapon (a damage bar shows over it when you're close) or catch it in a bomb blast; after many blows it collapses into a walkable heap and drops an AI key, a way into one AI's mainframe (more use for that to come). Kill the factory and its waves and repair drones stop. Topple a tower and the factory dispatches a revenge squad of W1 hunters, tougher and faster than a T2, but strictly melee — a hit needs one standing right on top of you, not just nearby. W1s attack in real waves — each one closes in and strikes, then falls back to regroup, then closes in again — and a squad spreads out to surround you rather than piling onto one spot. They track a position triangulated from the obelisk network, refreshed every few seconds rather than live, so even a jammed Wi-Fi block only slows them down, not stops them. The factory also fields fresh W1 waves on its own clock, an unarmed W3 repair drone whenever a damaged tower is left standing, and a W4 laser hunter-killer — unlike a W1 it never closes to melee, holding its range and firing on you instead, both the instant you attack an obelisk and, independently, every half hour of game time.

Out on the water, W2 droids hover in squads just above the river. Idle, they drift; but come near them and the whole squad turns and fires on you in waves. Their shots only reach you while you're in the water though — stay on dry land or a bridge and you can pick the squad off from the bank in safety.

Weapon strikes on any machine now throw a shower of sparks on impact.

Bombs

Scavenged timed bombs come in four sizes — small, medium, large, and a rare insane one. Hold a bomb and use it (/ or left click) to throw it, ticking, a good distance out — thrown in an arc, so it clears a wall or a low block between you and the target instead of stopping dead at the first one, useful for reaching a W4 that's holed up behind cover. Then get clear. When the fuse runs out it goes off in a cloud of fire that hurts everything nearby — animals, machines, droids, and you. The insane bomb is fierce enough to bring down an obelisk.

The world

A river divides the map; the bridges are the only crossings. Streams can be waded, slowly. Away from the towns the ground gets properly rugged — steep, tall hills and deep hollows, not just gentle rises — and high ground costs stamina to climb. Rubble and rocks can be climbed too, one step up like any other rise, and walked across the top of once you're up there — building walls stay solid, so a town or a house is still a real boundary, not just another slope. Somewhere in the ruins, the story of what happened here is waiting to be found. Sprayed slogans turn up here and there on the walls — some anti-machine, some signed by RON — and abandoned cars sit dead on the roads where the grid failed them.
