Books

Some buildings still hold books. Read one (R) and the knowledge is yours for the rest of the run. Woodcraft, herb lore, tracking, endurance: each book teaches a skill that changes how you survive.

Backpack

A rare find in the ruins. Once you have one it carries itself: 16 more slots and a spare-weapon slot, filled automatically once your pockets are full. Eating (Q) and firing a gun draw from it too, once your pockets run dry — no need to shuffle things forward. Press I to see what's in it.

Death is final

Dying restarts the game from defaults — score, skills, kills, everything, wiped clean, and a freshly shuffled world to go with it, exactly as if you had chosen New Game yourself. Clicking away the Certificate of Death does the reset; there is no continuing the run you just lost, only starting the next one. Winning is the one exception — dismissing a victory certificate just lets you carry on with what you've earned.

Staying alive

Food drains all the time and faster when you sprint. Find tinned food in the broken buildings and berries in the tall grass, and walk over loot to pocket it. Health only recovers when you are well fed; starve and it drains away. A felled tree drops wood — but not to a penknife: it's too flimsy to cut timber, and trying just tires you out and nicks your health. A saw, machete, or axe-like tool does the job. Nights are dark and dangerous: find torches in the buildings to carry a pool of light with you. An emptied resistance cache isn't gone for good either — RON quietly restocks one every so often with fresh batteries, ammo or shells.

Reloading the page without starting a new game isn't a clean reset either — it wipes your score and your entire obelisk kill record, just like a death would. Reload is only punished when it's used to duck out of the run you're already in.

The animals

Feral dogsHunt in packs; barking (!) means they have seen you. Hurt one and the pack backs off. Don't let them surround you.
BoarsPaw the ground, then charge dead straight. Step aside: a boar that hits a wall stuns itself.
VipersNear-invisible in tall grass; their venom drains health long after the bite. Where there are berries, there are snakes.
RavensThey don't bite; they tell everything else where you are, and they steal food. Keep to the trees.

The machines

The AIs still control this land. Their black obelisk towers pulse across the landscape, and their T-class hunters patrol around them.

T1A wheeled hunter, faster than your walk. It cannot climb: put any rise between you, or lure it into a hollow and it is trapped for good. Sprinting outruns it.
T2A biped stalker that walks exactly at your pace and does not tire. Sprint to break away, or stand and fight.

Every hunting machine — T1, T2, W1, W4 — genuinely needs to see you: break its line of sight behind a wall or a hill for a few seconds straight and it gives up the chase and wanders off, only re-noticing you later if you wander back within range. Hiding is a real tactic, not just running far enough.

Whatever is in your hand can be swung at them, even nothing at all — bare fists land a weak hit, a proper tool or blade lands a much harder one. Your penknife barely scratches them. RON — a resistance calling itself Reality or Nothing, whether or not it's still out there — hid weapons in crates inside the buildings: search them (E) for crowbars, bats, machetes, and if you are lucky the guns: a stun-gun (drops a machine cold for a while), an electro-gun (fuses one into a wreck you can mine for scrap), a pistol or a shotgun (holes in anything, but gunfire ruins the salvage). Guns are dead weight without batteries, ammo, or shells, hidden in the same crates. None of them shoot through a wall — a solid object blocks a shot exactly like it blocks a step. Pulling the trigger with nothing in your sights still fires — and still spends the round.

Every machine runs on a battery: hunting drains it and they crawl home to their obelisk to recharge. One that gets stuck runs flat where it stands. A flat machine can be reprogrammed (R, costs a battery) — it turns friendly, follows you, and a friendly T2 fells trees for you — or, if you'd rather just be rid of it, it can be destroyed like any other kill: it can't fight back while flat, and it still drops scrap.

The obelisks can be brought down. Carry a stun-gun, an electro-gun and a Wi-Fi block together and you're prompted to build an OB-gun (C): five burns topple a tower into a heap of circuit boards, batteries and scrap. A rare insane bomb can level one on its own. Every tower is assigned one of eight circuit numbers when the world is built, so toppling every obelisk always gets you a complete set of one to eight — collect them and you can build a wave gun (C), which fans laser fire through a whole crowd at once. Bring down every obelisk on the map and you win — the AI is defeated before SKYLINK ever wakes. Run out the countdown first and it wakes instead: every surviving tower lights up and links to its neighbours in a web of bright blue lasers, and the factory throws wave after wave of W4s at you — for good. There's no timer to survive to; play on for as long as you can, until it finally hunts you down.

Careful with the OB-gun's beam, though: fired at a robot rather than a tower, it doesn't kill — it corrupts the machine into a zombie, marked by a sickly green glow. A zombie shrugs off every weapon in the game except the bow and the wave gun; anything else just bounces off. W1 hunters (below) are already the AI's own revenge squad and can't be corrupted this way — the beam just damages them like anything else.

Somewhere on the map sits a W-factory: everything mechanical that isn't an obelisk-side T1/T2 is actually built there, not at the towers. Topple a tower and the factory dispatches a revenge squad of W1 hunters, tougher and faster than a T2, but strictly melee — a hit needs one standing right on top of you, not just nearby. W1s attack in real waves — each one closes in and strikes, then falls back to regroup, then closes in again — and a squad spreads out to surround you rather than piling onto one spot. They track a position triangulated from the obelisk network, refreshed every few seconds rather than live, so even a jammed Wi-Fi block only slows them down, not stops them. The factory also fields fresh W1 waves on its own clock, an unarmed W3 repair drone whenever a damaged tower is left standing, and a W4 laser hunter-killer — unlike a W1 it never closes to melee, holding its range and firing on you instead, both the instant you attack an obelisk and, independently, every half hour of game time.

Out on the water, W2 droids hover in squads just above the river. Idle, they drift; but come to the bank near them and the whole squad turns and fires on you in waves.

Weapon strikes on any machine now throw a shower of sparks on impact.

Bombs

Scavenged timed bombs come in four sizes — small, medium, large, and a rare insane one. Hold a bomb and use it (/ or left click) to throw it, ticking, a good distance out — thrown in an arc, so it clears a wall or a low block between you and the target instead of stopping dead at the first one, useful for reaching a W4 that's holed up behind cover. Then get clear. When the fuse runs out it goes off in a cloud of fire that hurts everything nearby — animals, machines, droids, and you. The insane bomb is fierce enough to bring down an obelisk.

The world

A river divides the map; the bridges are the only crossings. Streams can be waded, slowly. Away from the towns the ground gets properly rugged — steep, tall hills and deep hollows, not just gentle rises — and high ground costs stamina to climb. Rubble and rocks can be climbed too, one step up like any other rise, and walked across the top of once you're up there — building walls stay solid, so a town or a house is still a real boundary, not just another slope. Somewhere in the ruins, the story of what happened here is waiting to be found. Sprayed slogans turn up here and there on the walls — some anti-machine, some signed by RON — and abandoned cars sit dead on the roads where the grid failed them.
