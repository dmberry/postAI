# NostOS — a postAI Odyssey

**Version:** 1.55 · **Authors:** David and Henrik · **Started:** 4 July 2026 · **Repo:** https://github.com/dmberry/postAI · **Plans/suggestions:** [PAI-version-plan.md](PAI-version-plan.md)

*(Versioning policy: 0.01 increments from v0.3 onwards.)*

An isometric 2D survival game set in a world devastated by an AI takeover. Civilisation collapsed fighting the machines, and the machines are still here: black obelisk towers pulse across the landscape and T-class hunter robots patrol around them, hunting the humans that remain. Survivors scavenge the ruins while avoiding both the machines and wild animals that have gained strange powers (feral dog packs are a hazard to provoke, not a tripwire — crowd one and it turns, but a passing brush won't); a resistance calling itself **RON** — Reality or Nothing — hid weapons in caches through the broken towns, and their name still turns up sprayed on walls. Whether RON is still out there is never settled. How it all happened is never stated: the player pieces it together from newspapers, diaries, floppy disks, VHS tapes, and dead computers scattered through the world.

Inspired by Project Zomboid: knowledge is the real progression, scarcity drives movement, every fight is optional and risky, and the world tells the story.

## Current build (v1.55)

**The world:** a seeded 128x128 isometric map — a river with two bridges, a ten-building town, a ruined hamlet, forests, tall grass, and roads. Away from the towns the terrain gets properly rugged: steep hills and deep hollows, always climbable one step at a time. Rubble and rocks are low enough to step straight over; a **wall block** is taller, so it takes a **double jump** (press jump again in mid-air) to get on top — once up there you move a little slower for control, can roam the block tops, and just walk off any edge to drop back down and carry on. **Up on a block you're safe from ground attacks** — machines and animals can't reach you, so a block top is high ground to catch your breath (a bomb blast still finds you, and the flying machines to come will too). Building walls still stop you on foot, so a town or house is a real boundary until you deliberately climb it. The forests are drawn with proper hand-drawn tree art — mostly full leafy trees, with the odd small or bare/dead one — and a chopped tree shows a damage bar so you can see how many swings it has left. The map is ringed by impassable dark-gravel rock cliffs at its edge — drawn semi-transparent so you still see yourself if one stands between you and the camera. Streams can be waded; the river can be swum (only your head and shoulders show above the water), slowly and at a cost — both now carry a slow, gently travelling opacity ripple along the watercourse, a cheap stand-in for current without an actual scrolling texture. Day and night cycle, with genuinely dark nights and torches to push them back.

**Survival:** food, health, stamina, and venom all need managing. Health only recovers when fed and unpoisoned — on its own at a decent clip, or press **B** to lie down and rest — the screen dims, the clock visibly spins on, and health returns as you sleep — if you're hurt, off cooldown, and nothing's hunting you. Dying wipes score, skills, and kills and restarts the run from scratch, same as starting a New Game (**Ctrl+N**) yourself — but who you are doesn't reset: your chosen name and gender carry over into the new run. A bare **N** instead opens the notepad directly, no terminal needed.

**The machines:** black obelisk towers anchor wheeled **T1** hunters (can't climb — trap them in a hollow) and biped **T2** stalkers (match your walking pace exactly). A rare **T3** is a tactical ambusher rather than a chaser: it nests beside a tree near its tower and waits, with no proximity detection at all — only an actual clear line of sight counts as noticing you — then fires a heavy orange twin-laser volley (roughly double a W4's bolt) before a long recovery; crowd it and it only backs off enough to keep the shot lined up, or claws you at point-blank range. Topple a tower and the **W-factory** — a huge 8×8 foundry — answers with melee **W1** revenge squads and a ranged **W4** laser hunter-killer (which, if it can't hurt you through a shield or forcefield, stops plinking from range and bears right down on you instead); unarmed **W3** drones repair damaged obelisks left standing (or, if one's been pinned by the `loop` hack below, work the loop back out and release it and its garrison); a **W5** gardener drone — the factory keeps roughly one out in the world at all times — wanders unarmed and harmlessly plants saplings as it goes; **W2** droids patrol the river — but their shots can only reach you while you're in the water, so pick them off from the bank. The factory itself can be brought down, but only by real force: a heavy anti-machine tool (crowbar, sledgehammer, or robot sword), explosives, or the electro-gun — a penknife or a light blade just rings off the hull. Hammer it with the right kit (a damage bar shows when you're near) and after many blows it collapses, spilling an **AI key** — a way into one AI's mainframe, ahead of the terminal-hacking to come. Every hunting machine needs genuine line of sight — break it behind cover and it gives up (T3 takes this furthest, needing sight to notice you at all, not just to keep hunting). Crossing a slope costs a machine effort too, same as it costs you stamina — expect any of them to slow down climbing or descending a height step. Machines never overlap each other's tile — a crowd spreads out automatically — and a collision between two of them chips both, so a jammed-together squad is quietly hurting itself. All of them run on a battery: a drained one goes flat and inert, and can either be **reprogrammed** (**R**, costs a battery — it fights for you) or just destroyed for scrap if you'd rather not bother. They value their chassis too: batter one down below about a fifth of its health and it breaks off and **limps home to mend at its charger** — slowly, so a routed machine is genuinely out of the fight for a while — before rejoining its rounds. And a wreck only ever sheds what the machine actually carried: batteries, scrap, chip fragments, the odd prize — never ordnance it had no way to throw. New runs go easier on you at first: the game reads pace of movement (not combat) to spot a player still finding their feet, and quietly shortens machine detection range and damage while that lasts — it fades back to full strength the moment your movement shows you've settled in, or after a few minutes regardless.

**Combat & weapons:** a full armoury from the penknife up through swords, guns, and a railgun, viewable with a power rating in the Armoury (**V**) as you find each one. Bombs come in four sizes and stack in a pocket; they land on the tile under your cursor (out to their throw range). Ten scrap can be beaten into a **robot sword** (press **C**), a heavy anti-machine blade; the **bow** is a hard-hitting long-range option. Melee and gun practice both build XP over time. Every ranged weapon fires by line of sight and stops at a solid wall; pulling the trigger with nothing in view still spends the round. The **OB-gun** brings down obelisk towers (five burns to fell one, with a damage bar over the tower as it scorches — W3 drones repair the damage if left standing, so it takes the heavy kit; a felled tower always spills an **access chip** among its salvage, so bringing one down hands you the means to jack into the others) or, fired at a robot instead, simply **wipes the machine out where it stands**, whatever its class (at the price of a battery per shot and most of the salvage); the **wave gun** fans laser fire through a crowd. The **electro-gun** now runs on a self-charging internal cell (worth four batteries) that trickles back up while you carry it — it destroys a machine outright in a shower of sparks, scares any nearby animals into fleeing, and its arc even scorches **obelisks** (a slower way to fell a tower than the OB-gun, but it works). On defence there are **shields** that protect while simply carried, no need to hold them: a riot shield absorbs a laser, a **mirror shield** bounces it straight back to destroy the shooter, and a rare battery-hungry **forcefield** wraps you in a green shell nothing gets through at all — a shimmering energy shell (a soft glow rimmed with a travelling dashed edge) shows around you whenever a shield is up. The forcefield is the one exception that needs a deliberate **click** (in whichever slot it's sitting in) to arm; click again to disarm and stop the drain. Landing a melee hit now **knocks the target back** and rattles it for a beat, Minecraft-style, so it can't just stand there trading blows and out-damaging you the instant your swing connects.

**Story & progression:** books teach permanent skills (woodcraft, herbalism, tracking, fleet foot). Carry the **electro-compass** and **click it** to arm it — it stays on until you drop it, and your aim chevron becomes a small cluster of homing needles, one per nearest notable thing, colour-coded (blue factory, green obelisk, yellow dropped backpack, orange dropped OB-gun). Each obelisk has a little **terminal screen** you can click (only the screen itself, not the tower body) to jack in — but you need an **access chip** (carried, not held) to use it: with the chip, a connect bar opens a channel into a live green console, and while you're logged in the obelisk hides you from the machines. Click a terminal **without** a chip and you get the AI's own OS instead — a magenta wall of restless, unreadable data you can't touch. That console runs **RON-ML** ([design](docs/ob-terminal-language.md)), a tiny functional language the resistance left scattered through the ruins as runnable fragments: `scan` lists nearby obelisks, `scan |> nearest` pipes that to the closest one. The easy way in needs no key at all: `loop node` pins an infinite loop into it, freezing the node and its garrison where they stand (its light burns white-hot and it starts smoking, more heavily the longer it runs) until a repair drone eventually works the loop back out. With an **AI key** (dropped by a destroyed W-factory) you can go further: `hack node` then `crash node key` knocks a tower properly dark for a while; `rewind t` claws `t` hours back off the POSEIDON deadline clock, bought time rather than a fight (only before the purge actually starts). `map` pulls up a schematic of the whole AI territory — obelisks, machines, the factory, and the **mainframe** you're ultimately hunting for; `print` runs off a physical copy that drops at your feet, so you can carry the map and unfold it anywhere (just click it in your inventory). Type `eliza` (or `run eliza`) and the node loads **ELIZA**, Weizenbaum's 1966 DOCTOR script — a working reconstruction of the original Rogerian chatbot you can actually talk to at the prompt (`quit`, or Ctrl+C, to leave); the machine reading a human the way the machines read everything. The obelisks aren't the only terminals: up on the **hilltops** stand **TOR relays**, RON's own pre-collapse field stations, each with a warm-amber **HERMES** console (the counter-name to the AIs' TIRESIAS — Hermes is the god who hands Odysseus the herb *moly* to break Circe's enchantment). HERMES needs no chip and no key — it was never theirs — and it's a **separate system** from the obelisks, with its own commands (the AI-network verbs simply aren't part of it, and vice versa). It's an **information resource**, not a workshop: RON built it to keep the human record alive. `archive` lists the documents it holds; `read <topic>` shows one on the terminal (the RON-ML reference, a fortress schematic, notes on machine technology, the history of the takeover, RON's own notes on destroying the AIs, vector theory); `print <topic>` runs off a copy filed straight into your notepad (**N**) so you carry the knowledge away. It runs off its own solar cell — a gauge in the terminal you watch wear down as you use it and creep back up in the sun. And `drive` **overrides a nearby machine**: you steer the unit around the ordinary isometric map while a robot-vision panel (top-right — an ASCII sensor feed with hull, link-range and cell gauges) shows the scene through its eyes and brackets everything near it; walk it out of the relay's range or trip its self-destruct (**X**), or press **Esc** to let it go. Type `help` for a command reference (or `help <verb>` for one), and once you've found and read **the RON-DOS Operator's Manual** (a book in a cache, with torn pages scattered around), the console autocompletes verbs for you as faded ghost text — press **Tab** to accept. Every page worth keeping — a fragment that teaches an actual command, a found transcript, whatever else earns the flag — copies itself into your **notepad** as you find it (a general page-collector, not RON-ML-specific) — type `notes` at any terminal, or just press **N** anywhere, to open it as a real page you flip through (arrow keys or the on-screen buttons), not a wall of console text. Bad commands get a hint on what to try instead. There's a secret word too, never written down whole anywhere — find it and the terminal lets go of you so you can watch what happens. A battered can of **Ubik** (Philip K. Dick's reality-spray) turns up somewhere in the ruins: each spray brightens a patch of ground and everything on it, fading back to normal after a while, with an occasional PKD-flavoured ad line instead of the plain message and a localised flicker/discolour/lean/twist hiccup right where you sprayed. Spray the same spot three times running and it doesn't just brighten — it **tears clean through reality** and holds open. Step into the tear and you fall out of the world entirely, into the **Backspace**: a jaundiced, fluorescent-lit block of huge liminal rooms — faded yellows, worn and stained lino, furniture stacked around at random, rooms joined only by doorways, distant sounds of something moving — with one pale, wrong thing wandering the far rooms that only notices you if it actually sees you. The way home is right where you arrive: a second tear signed **EXIT** in fire-exit green, a few steps into the first room, so you can always get out; wandering deeper toward the thing is the optional risk. Any hunting machine that wanders into a brightened patch (or the mouth of a tear) loses its mind for as long as it lingers there — spinning, juddering, taking wild swings at whatever other machine strays close, no longer hunting you at all. Lore fragments scattered through the ruins build into a **Scrapbook** (**J**) — newspapers, diaries, disks, tapes glued in at a slight tilt like real pasted-in clippings, one book, not a plain list — that never quite states what happened, only lets you assemble it, including scraps of a lost pre-collapse argument about "vector theory" (what the machines actually think in), the fallen **Magnifica Humanitas** project, a much older machine intelligence — a century-old therapy-program trick and the man who built it and spent the rest of his life warning people off it, referenced by name (including, verbatim, the actual historical transcript that made the trick famous) — and the real academic history behind the console's own name. RON graffiti (and rarer UBIK, Magnifica Humanitas — now the second most common theme after RON itself — and half-remembered vector slogans, plus the rarest of all — a real weathered poster photo stuck to some walls instead of paint) and abandoned cars (real 3/4-view sprites — classic saloons in several colours, a police car, an ambulance — oriented along whichever road they're parked on rather than a random angle) litter the world for texture. Resistance caches restock over time, capped at five per building so no single house gets flooded with loot while others sit empty; the nearest one to spawn is a hand-picked welcome kit (backpack, shield, a ranged weapon, food) that pulses with a white glow while you still read as a beginner, fading once you don't. Loot you find placed in the world — in caches and scattered through buildings — waits for you and never rots; only what's dropped *during play* decays off the ground at its own pace, to keep the world from silting up with salvage: meat and berries go quickly, scrap and ammo linger a while, dropped weapons last longest, and a few irreplaceable things (the Wi-Fi block, the AI key, circuit boards, a backpack) never vanish at all.

**Character & UI:** the game opens on a **title screen** — the NostOS logo over the dancing machines and the playable Walkman soundtrack, with **Continue** (resume your save) and **New game** buttons; clicking either stops the title music and drops you into the world. The title (and the mobile gate) Walkman has proper **play/pause, stop and next-track** buttons, and tapping a tape starts it — tap it again to skip to the next track. Play as Adam, Eve, or Neve (or a custom name), now rendered as a directional pixel-art sprite with a real walk cycle that turns to face wherever you aim. Backpack (**I**), skills (**K**), Armoury (**V**), and Scrapbook (**J**) all close on **H** — or by clicking away from the panel, same as the help modal. Pockets and the hands slot are draggable straight off the compact dashboard, not just the full backpack panel — drag an item off any slot to drop it on the ground, or onto another slot to move it. Dying (or winning) shows a shareable Certificate of Death, now on a weathered stone-textured backing with a small portrait of who this was. The game starts on a sparse synthesised piano bed; **M** cycles the background music through that, three real found-tape tracks by meme, and off — and it plays straight through a fight now instead of ducking to silence the moment something attacks. The found tapes are also yours to start yourself on the **walkman on a carry strap**, right on the dashboard: it takes **cassette tapes** (you start with one — "meme", resonance on side A, slip on side B; more get seeded into the world over time), and clicking the tape cycles play side A, flip to side B, stop — the reels visibly turning and the artist/track scrolling across a little screen while it plays. Starting a tape loops it over the synth bed; stopping it drops back to the synth. A **Settings** tab in the help modal (**?**) offers the same music choice directly, plus a volume slider, both remembered across sessions — the home for more options as they're added. The **i** button next to help opens an About box: coding, music, and the ideas/influences behind the game.

**Win condition:** a countdown runs to POSEIDON's completion. Destroy every obelisk before it finishes and you win; run out the clock instead and every surviving tower links up for an escalating W4 onslaught. Even then it isn't hopeless: felling a tower mid-purge collapses the POSEIDON web and shuts it down for a reprieve, until a repair drone reaches the wreck and raises the tower again. Knock them down faster than they can be rebuilt and you can still win outright during the purge.

**Still queued (large systems):** a mobile phone + RON text tips.

## Running

No build tools, no dependencies. Serve the folder and open it:

```
python3 -m http.server 8000
# then open http://localhost:8000
```

(Opening `index.html` directly also works in browsers that allow ES modules from `file://`; a local server is the reliable route.)

## Controls

The full, current control list is in-game: press **H** (thematically organised into Movement & camera, Combat & tools, Survival, Menus & info, and System). The essentials to get moving:

- **WASD / arrow keys**: move · **Mouse**: aim (you always face the cursor) · **Shift**: sprint · **Space**: jump
- **E / / / left click**: use the held tool
- **H**: help (also closes by clicking away from the panel)

## Tech

- HTML5 Canvas 2D, plain JavaScript ES modules
- 2:1 isometric tiles, painter's-algorithm depth sorting
- Chunk-friendly renderer that only draws the visible tile range
- Autosave to `localStorage`

## Layout

```
index.html          entry point
src/main.js         bootstrap + fixed-timestep game loop
src/engine/         iso maths, renderer, camera, input
src/game/           tiles, map, player, robots, animals, lore (game content)
```
